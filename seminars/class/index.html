14 Aug 2012

Noon, Eck 312

Charles Staats

The Plücker embedding describes the Grassmannian as a closed subvariety of projective space. I will explain (in very simple terms) how to go from linearly independent vectors $v_1, \dotsc, v_k$ in $n$-space to the point in projective space representing the vector space $\text{Span}(v_1, \dotsc, v_k) \in G(k,n)$. (This will take about two minutes.) I will then proceed to more confusing issues: Why does this map depend only on the span of the $v_i$? Why is it injective on the Grassmannian? Why is the its image a closed subvariety of projective space? The answers will involve factorization in the exterior algebra (considered as a ring).

If I have time, I address a somewhat less basic question: What do lines contained in the Grassmannian look like? (They turn out to be the Schubert varieties of dimension $1$, but you won't need to know what that means to understand my description.)

This talk will be at a basic level, perhaps more so than the relevant passage in Joe Harris's book "First Course" (which, to be honest, I find a little confusing on this particular topic). The primary tool used will be exterior algebra.

01 Aug 2012

Noon, Eck 312

Charles Staats

The intersection theory for projective space is so simple as to be almost boring, at least in its statement: the intersection ring is generated, as a ring, by the class $[H]$ of a hyperplane. The class $[H]^k$ is represented by the intersection of $k$ general hyperplanes. The only relation is that when you intersect too many hyperplanes, you get zero.

Like projective space, Grassmannians have an intersection ring that is well understood, with a standard basis represented by geometrically motivated subvarieties, in this case called Schubert varieties. Unlike in the case of projective space, the intersection ring of Grassmannians is complex enough to give non-obvious geometric information about these subvarieties. In my first talk, I will work out one or more examples of intersection rings for Grassmannians. I will avoid being very technical; most of the computations will be "by picture." I should get to at least one result that is not obvious, at least to me:

Q: If you have four lines in general position in $\mathbb{P}^3$, describe the set of lines intersecting all four of them.
A: There are exactly two such lines.

On Thursday, I will hopefully be a little bit more systematic, if not rigorous, in describing the intersection rings for Grassmannians.

Simion Filip: Belyi's theorem

Suppose $X$ is an algebraic curve defined over a number field. Belyi's theorem says that it admits a map to $\mathbb{P}^1$, ramified only over $0$,$1$ and $\infty$. I'll explain a proof of this, why the converse is "trivial", and hopefully why this is at all interesting.

27 Jun 2012

Noon, Eck 312

Preston Wake

In number theory, some of the oldest questions are about Diophantine equations: given a set of algebraic equations with integer coefficients, how many solutions are there in the integers? These questions are very difficult in general, but a necessary condition is that the solution modulo a prime $p$ should give a solution to the equations modulo $p$. So a simpler question is: given a set of equations over a finite field, how many solutions are there in the finite field? Do I get more solutions if I allow my solutions to live in a field extension? How many more?

To answer these questions, we can form the zeta function associated to the problem, which is a kind of generating function for the sequence $a_n=\#\{\text{solutions in }\mathbb{F}_{p^n}\}$. When the set of equations cut out a smooth proper algebraic variety, Andre Weil made some very deep conjectures about this zeta function, and proved his conjectures in some simple cases. Grothendieck and his school proved all but one of these conjectures using ideas from algebraic topology. I will explain their basic argument. Deligne then provided a proof of the remaining conjecture, the Riemann Hypothesis, in a way which shockingly (and immorally?) used analysis. I will explain the basic idea of his proof in the case of an elliptic curve—there will be pictures.

Noon, Eck 312

Asilata Bapat

The Littlewood-Richardson coefficients are certain structure constants that appear in the representation theory of the symmetric group, as well in several other problems. In this talk, I will present some of the mathematics surrounding these numbers, and links between the different questions they answer. Among other things, I will talk about representations of $GL_n$ and Schur polynomials.

13 Jun 2012

Noon, Eck 312

Daniel Le

The Mordell-Weil theorem states that the $\mathbb{Q}$-points of an elliptic curve defined over $\mathbb{Q}$ form a finitely generated abelian group. A theorem of Mazur classifies the possible torsion parts of these groups. In this talk, I'll explain why an elliptic curve defined over $\mathbb{Q}$ has no 11-torsion defined over $\mathbb{Q}$.

Possible topics

Algebraic Geometry:

  • Examples from Harris' Algebraic Geometry: A First Course, Hartshorne chapter 1
  • Examples from Harris and Morrison's Moduli of curves
  • Examples from Eisenbud and Harris' 3264 and more (available online): 27 lines on a cubic (see also Hartshorne Theorem V.4.9), 3264 conics
  • Examples from Hartshorne chapters 4 and 5: Hurwitz bound (exercise IV.2.5), Pascal's theorem (exercise IV.4.5) and the Weil bound (exercise V.1.10)
  • Poncelet's porism
  • Bertini's theorem (see Hartshorne II.8.18)
  • Riemann-Roch and applications (classification of curves)
  • Sheaf cohomology and computations of Hodge numbers
  • Blow-ups as incomplete linear systems, basepoints, examples of blowups: the cubic surface, the quadratic transformation of P^2 (see Hartshorne V.4)
  • Castelnuovo's criterion (Hartshorne Theorem V.5.7)
  • Surface singularities (Kleinian singularities), Neron polygons, Dynkin diagrams

Number theory:

  • Elliptic curves: anything from Tate's The Arithmetic of Elliptic Curves (particularly section 4) or Silverman's The Arithmetic of Elliptic Curves
  • Tate modules, Neron-Ogg-Shafarevich criterion
  • Proof that the group law is associative (see Silverman or Miles Reid's Undergraduate Algebraic Geometry)
  • Complex multiplication (see Silverman's Advanced Topics in the Arithmetic of Elliptic Curves)
  • Modular forms (see Serre's A Course in Arithmetic or many other books): examples, Ramanujan's congruences, four squares, Hecke operators, Galois representations
  • Modular forms as line bundles and/or as automorphic forms
  • Quadratic reciprocity, class field theory, reciprocity laws, and the Langlands program
  • Quadratic forms and representability

Representation theory:

  • Geometric plethysms (see Fulton and Harris)
  • Highest weight theory
  • Schur-Weyl duality
  • The Bruhat decomposition, linear algebra, Schubert cells
  • The Iwasawa decomposition
  • The Cartan decomposition and elementary divisors
  • The RSK correspondence
